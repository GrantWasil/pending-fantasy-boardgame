# Mixed-motivation table dynamics

Research for [Investigate mixed-motivation table dynamics](https://github.com/GrantWasil/pending-fantasy-boardgame/issues/5), conducted 2026-07-20.

## Executive answer

The credible evidence supports a **conditional, pluralist model of table behavior**, not a list of universal player types or solved design rules.

- **Mixed motives are real.** In a large hobby-board-gamer survey, social interaction and strategising were the highest-rated motives, but competition remained materially important. Laboratory work also finds heterogeneous and conditional cooperation. This supports designing for players who can care about winning, relationships, fairness, competence, and the shared event at the same time. It does not establish how a particular friend group will rank those motives in a particular game.
- **Perceived defeat is not equivalent to disengagement.** Competitive-game studies support suspense from honest outcome uncertainty and the motivational value of competence feedback. They also show that losers can persist for ego-involved reasons, while broader goal-regulation research finds that disengaging from a genuinely unattainable goal and reengaging elsewhere can be adaptive. The evidence does not justify keeping players in false hope of victory at all costs.
- **Alliance persistence is plausible, but not inevitable.** Conditional cooperation, loyalty costs, reciprocity, friendship, and visible group identity can all make a relationship matter beyond its immediate payoff. Counterevidence shows that induced identity can have no effect when behavior and history are unobservable. No primary study located here establishes that tabletop alliances naturally become permanent or that forced expiration reliably improves them.
- **Betrayal attribution is well-supported.** People often react differently when another person, rather than chance, produces an equivalent bad outcome; perceived intent and foreseeability increase blame. Costly retaliation is common in economic games, but its motive may be norm enforcement, equality, spite, or relationship-coordinated negative reciprocity. An attack's payoff effect alone is therefore an inadequate measure of its social meaning.
- **“The game made me do it” is a credible hypothesis, not a finding.** Labels and frames can change cooperation by changing beliefs about the situation, but those effects can vanish under different action sequences or payoff structures. No direct experiment located here shows that objective-driven aggression at a friend table is blamed less than strategically identical discretionary aggression.
- **Kingmaking has no universal empirical verdict.** The available behavioral evidence can explain several motives for spoiler behavior, while first-party design evidence shows that respected designers intentionally use it as consequential agency. I did not locate controlled research establishing when mixed-motivation tabletop groups experience kingmaking as drama, fairness correction, retaliation, favoritism, or illegitimate play. That boundary is partly a table norm and must be specified and tested.

The practical conclusion is to treat the handoff's claims as hypotheses about an **interaction ecology**. Research can identify mechanisms and risks, but representative intact friend groups and integrated prototypes are needed to determine how these mechanisms combine. As the companion note on [emergence-aware tabletop prototyping](./emergence-aware-tabletop-prototyping.md) argues, isolated probes cannot validate session-level social dynamics.

## Evidence standard and scope

This note separates four kinds of support:

1. **Established within the studied setting:** a result reported by peer-reviewed primary research, bounded by that study's participants, task, incentives, and duration.
2. **Transfer inference:** a design implication inferred from a laboratory, digital-game, or non-game result. It is plausible but not validated for a long multiplayer tabletop session.
3. **First-party design precedent:** a published rulebook, designer talk, or development diary showing that a design deliberately uses a structure. It proves the structure exists and records the creator's rationale; it does not prove a general causal effect.
4. **Unresolved or group-specific:** a claim for which direct evidence was not located, evidence conflicts, or the likely outcome depends on table norms, relationships, culture, and the rest of the rules.

The closest direct population evidence is a survey of hobby board gamers. Most causal evidence comes from short laboratory economic games or competitive digital games. Those abstractions are valuable because they isolate attribution, uncertainty, reciprocity, or framing, but they do not reproduce a multi-hour game among friends with negotiation, history, physical presence, and several interacting systems.

## What the evidence establishes

### 1. Social and competitive motives coexist; behavior is not a fixed persona

Cross and colleagues surveyed 1,603 hobby board gamers recruited through board-game social groups and a distributor's channels. Median importance ratings on 0–100 sliders were social interaction 80, strategising 78, escapism 69, skill building 67, and competition 64. Player elimination was the least-enjoyed mechanism in their survey. The result directly establishes that this self-selected hobbyist sample valued several motives simultaneously at the aggregate level; it does not recover stable motivation profiles for each player or predict behavior at a particular table. The sample was also predominantly male, white, educated, and already active in the hobby. ([Cross et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1177/10468781231189493))

Laboratory social-dilemma research independently rejects a single-motive model. In a one-shot public-goods experiment, about half of participants gave contribution schedules that rose with others' contributions, while about one third free-rode. The categories describe choices in one elicitation task, not immutable personality types. ([Fischbacher, Gächter, and Fehr, 2001](https://www.econ.uzh.ch/dam/jcr%3Affffffff-9758-127f-0000-00003449356a/ArePeopleCondCooperative.pdf)) Social-value-orientation experiments likewise distinguish concern for personal advantage, joint outcomes, and equality, and find that prosocial participants can be particularly reciprocal rather than unconditionally cooperative. ([Van Lange, 1999](https://doi.org/10.1037/0022-3514.77.2.337))

Situation also changes behavior. Identical repeated Prisoner's Dilemma tasks labeled “Community Game” and “Wall Street Game” produced substantially different cooperation in two populations, while reputation-based predictions did not distinguish cooperators from defectors. ([Liberman, Samuels, and Ross, 2004](https://doi.org/10.1177/0146167204264004)) A later set of experiments replicated the label effect in simultaneous play but found it vanished when only one participant controlled the outcome and when play was sequential, supporting a belief-coordination explanation rather than a wholesale change in preference. ([Ellingsen et al., 2012](https://scholar.harvard.edu/sites/scholar.harvard.edu/files/jmollers/files/1-s2.0-s0899825612000802-main.pdf))

**Design inference:** do not assign players stable identities such as “social player” and “competitive player” and expect the rules merely to satisfy each camp. Model the current decision as a collision among victory, fairness, loyalty, competence, retaliation, and shared-experience motives. State, framing, observability, and expectations can change which motive governs the move.

### 2. Defeat, suspense, competence, and disengagement are distinct

In two studies using a competitive zero-sum digital game, closer scores increased enjoyment through suspense even though wide-margin wins produced higher perceived competence. Participants later chose games they had rated as higher-suspense and lower-competence over lower-suspense, higher-competence games; concern about performance moderated that preference. ([Abuhamdeh, Csikszentmihalyi, and Jalal, 2015](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11031-014-9425-2)) This supports **honest outcome uncertainty** as one source of enjoyment. It does not show that obscuring an already-decided outcome, adding arbitrary catch-up effects, or pretending every seat can still win will have the same effect.

A separate competition experiment found winners more intrinsically motivated than losers. Among losers, an explicit performance standard plus positive feedback for meeting it improved intrinsic motivation relative to losing without that competence signal. Yet losers also showed more ego-involved persistence than winners or non-competitors. ([Vansteenkiste and Deci, 2003](https://www.selfdeterminationtheory.org/SDT/documents/2003_MaartenDeci.pdf)) Thus continued effort is not a clean measure of enjoyment, and losing does not automatically yield behavioral withdrawal.

Broader self-regulation evidence points in the opposite direction from “never let the player concede the goal.” Across three correlational studies, the capacity to disengage from unattainable goals and reengage in valued alternatives was associated with higher subjective well-being. ([Wrosch et al., 2003](https://www.cmu.edu/dietrich/psychology/pdf/scales/GAS_article.pdf)) That is not a game study, but it is counterevidence to treating goal abandonment itself as pathological.

**Design inference:** distinguish at least four states in testing:

- `uncertain`: the player can still plausibly win and meaningful choices change that probability;
- `behind but agentic`: winning is unlikely, but the player can still demonstrate competence and alter their own result;
- `victory-unattainable but reengaged`: the player has a consequential, self-owned alternative goal;
- `disengaged`: the player no longer considers available decisions meaningful.

The provisional design target should be meaningful agency and competence under honest uncertainty, not permanent belief in victory. Alternative goals may help after victory becomes unattainable, but they require integrated testing because they can also create arbitrary spoiler incentives.

### 3. Alliances can acquire social weight beyond current payoff

Several mechanisms make alliance inertia plausible:

- Conditional cooperators respond to others' cooperation rather than only to current material optimization. ([Fischbacher, Gächter, and Fehr, 2001](https://www.econ.uzh.ch/dam/jcr%3Affffffff-9758-127f-0000-00003449356a/ArePeopleCondCooperative.pdf))
- A model tested with two dynamic games in a Pakistani factory found evidence consistent with loyalty imposing a psychological cost on defecting from ongoing cooperation, distinguishable from reciprocity and complementary to guilt aversion. The effect was stronger within than between groups. ([Foucart and Tan, 2024](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11238-023-09966-4))
- Minimal-group experiments found that arbitrary categorization could produce ingroup-favoring allocations and even preference for maximizing the ingroup–outgroup difference over some objective advantage. ([Tajfel et al., 1971](https://doi.org/10.1002/ejsp.2420010202))
- In a three-player power-to-take experiment, responders who were friends punished a proposer more and coordinated that punishment without communication more often than strangers; emotional reactions explained both effects. ([van Winden, 2008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jpubeco.2007.04.012))

None of these results makes alliance persistence automatic. In an indefinitely repeated helping-game experiment, randomly assigned color identities reinforced by an initial in-group task produced no later group bias when participants met as strangers and could not observe personal identities, personal histories, or group behavior. ([Camera and Hohl, 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jebo.2021.06.017)) The contrast makes observability and shared history part of the causal mechanism, not incidental details.

**Design inference:** a prior alliance can remain meaningful because breaking it sacrifices identity, reciprocity, reputation, or a shared story, even when betrayal is locally profitable. This is a reason to make alliance history and transitions legible. It is not evidence that alliances must expire, that they should be costless to break, or that a scheduled renegotiation window will necessarily make politics more fluid.

### 4. Betrayal is partly about agency and intent, not only loss

In payoff-matched trust and risk tasks, participants generally required a higher acceptable probability of success when another person's choice rather than nature determined the outcome. The effect appeared in an initial experiment and in a six-country study, which the authors interpreted as betrayal aversion. ([Bohnet and Zeckhauser, 2004](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jebo.2003.11.004); [Bohnet et al., 2008](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257%2Faer.98.1.294)) The mechanism is not settled: a later 330-participant experiment replicated the aggregate contrast but found that reluctance to delegate decision rights contributed to it and that its treatment effect appeared only among men in that sample. ([Humphrey and Mondorf, 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.econlet.2020.109663))

Attribution research supplies a more direct reason to track intent. Experiments using adverse causal chains found intentional and foreseeable actions were judged more causal and blameworthy than unintentional or less foreseeable ones. ([Lagnado and Channon, 2008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cognition.2008.06.009)) Further experiments found that perceived intentional harm increased participants' effort and persistence in blaming, condemning, and punishing. ([Ames and Fiske, 2015](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4378403/)) These are non-game scenarios, so the tabletop transfer remains an inference, but they establish that objective harm magnitude does not exhaust blame.

Costly punishment is also behaviorally robust but motivationally ambiguous. Public-goods experiments found participants would pay to punish defectors even without material gain, and cooperation increased when punishment was available. ([Fehr and Gächter, 2002](https://doi.org/10.1038/415137a)) A reanalysis of the same data argued that egalitarian punishment of high earners explained more than punishment of noncooperation. ([Fowler, Johnson, and Smirnov, 2005](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature03256)) Comparable experiments across 16 participant pools found substantial antisocial punishment of high contributors and major cross-societal variation; in some pools it erased punishment's cooperation benefit. ([Herrmann, Thöni, and Gächter, 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18323447/)) Friendship can amplify coordinated negative reciprocity rather than soften it. ([van Winden, 2008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jpubeco.2007.04.012))

**Design inference:** when a player attacks a former ally or helps decide the winner, record the player's stated reason and the target's attribution. The same move may be read as optimal play, norm enforcement, equality restoration, revenge, loyalty to somebody else, or extra-game favoritism. Board position and target choice alone cannot distinguish them.

### 5. Game-legitimized aggression is plausible but unproven

Call aggression **game-legitimized** when a visible role, objective, or rule makes the harmful action appear required or invited by the game rather than freely chosen from personal preference.

Three established findings make the concept plausible:

1. Situational labels can coordinate different expectations and change cooperation, so rules text and objective framing can affect behavior. ([Liberman, Samuels, and Ross, 2004](https://doi.org/10.1177/0146167204264004); [Ellingsen et al., 2012](https://scholar.harvard.edu/sites/scholar.harvard.edu/files/jmollers/files/1-s2.0-s0899825612000802-main.pdf))
2. Perceived intent and foreseeability change blame, so a credible external constraint could change attribution. ([Lagnado and Channon, 2008](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cognition.2008.06.009))
3. When people can keep the connection between their choice and another person's outcome ambiguous, self-interested behavior can increase—the “moral wiggle room” effect. ([Dana, Weber, and Kuang, 2007](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00199-006-0153-z))

The first two findings do **not** establish that objective cover reduces resentment. The third warns that cover could increase aggression while merely helping the actor preserve a fair self-image. Effects also depend on strategic structure: positive framing increased cooperation in a linear public-good game but not in nonlinear public-good or common-pool-resource games where best responses opposed the frame. ([Isaksen, Brekke, and Richter, 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jeem.2018.11.005))

The handoff claim should therefore be split into separate hypotheses:

- objective framing increases the likelihood of an aggressive action;
- the target attributes less personal intent to that action;
- the target assigns less blame and reports less relationship harm;
- the action still reads as strategically meaningful rather than arbitrary compulsion.

Those outcomes may move in different directions. A valid test needs a small interaction sandbox with the same payoffs and strategic consequences under objective-framed and discretionary conditions, plus intact friend groups and separate actor/target measures. An isolated choice frequency is insufficient.

### 6. Kingmaking is a structural possibility whose legitimacy is unresolved

For this project, an operational definition should be explicit: **a player who no longer has a plausible route to victory intentionally changes which other player wins**. Other definitions include actions by any non-leading player or require that the action not improve the actor's own standing, so studies and playtest reports are not comparable without stating the boundary.

I did not locate controlled primary research on kingmaking in long-form multiplayer tabletop games. The studies above establish candidate mechanisms—negative reciprocity, equality seeking, loyalty, group favoritism, and social framing—but cannot tell whether the resulting move will be experienced as legitimate play.

The clearest first-party design argument is Cole Wehrle's GDC talk, which defends designing for kingmaking because player disruption and the ability to bolster another player can create complex positions and dramatic narratives. ([Wehrle, “King Me,” 2019](https://www.gdcvault.com/play/1025683/board-game-design-day-king)) During *Oath*'s development, Wehrle wrote that kingmaking opportunities were abundant and reported greater tolerance for them when every player's actions shaped a consequential chronicle beyond the current winner. ([Wehrle, “Oath Designer Diary 8,” 2019](https://ledergames.com/blogs/news/oath-designer-diary-8-destinations-and-paths-victory-part-3)) These are expert rationales and development observations, not controlled evidence or universal preference.

**Design inference:** “kingmaking is bad” and “kingmaking is agency” are both underspecified. The project must decide which forms it wants to legitimize—for example, strategically earned leverage or retaliation grounded in the game—and which it wants to discourage, such as arbitrary extra-game favoritism. Then testing should measure whether target players recognize that distinction.

## First-party design precedents, not validation

Existing games demonstrate several ways to structure and frame these dynamics:

| Game or source | Deliberate structure | What it proves—and does not prove |
| --- | --- | --- |
| [*Dune* rulebook](https://www.gf9games.com/dunegame/wp-content/uploads/Dune-Rulebook.pdf) | Alliances are public, limited to two players, share a victory condition, prevent allied battle, and may form or break only during event-triggered Nexus windows. A player who breaks may immediately join another alliance. | Proves scheduled, public renegotiation and shared victory are implementable precedents. It does not establish that windows prevent alliance inertia or improve every table's experience. |
| [*Cosmic Encounter* rulebook](https://images-cdn.fantasyflightgames.com/ffg_content/Cosmic%20Encounter/cosmic-encounter-rulebook.pdf) and [quick-start guide](https://images-cdn.fantasyflightgames.com/filer_public/a8/bf/a8bfc766-193c-4f40-a4ea-9864b8ae7953/ce01_rulebook_web.pdf) | Main players invite allies afresh for each encounter; invitees may accept one side or neither. The quick-start explicitly frames the competitive game as a social party game and asks players to value diplomacy, surprising outcomes, and role-play. | Proves temporary coalitions can be rebuilt at encounter scale and that first-party rules can set a social norm. It does not establish that the norm is adopted or that losing becomes enjoyable for every group. |
| [*Diplomacy* rulebook](https://retailers.renegadegamestudios.com/content/File%20Storage%20for%20site/Rulebooks/Diplomacy/DiplomacyRGS_Rulebook_v6_LR.pdf) | Every turn includes a negotiation phase; alliances, promises kept, and promises broken are described as the heart of play, while the winner must ultimately stand alone. | Proves betrayal can be explicitly included in the game's declared social contract. It does not show that friendship effects disappear or that all players accept every betrayal. |
| [*Twilight Imperium: Fourth Edition* overview](https://www.fantasyflightgames.com/en/news/2017/8/11/twilight-imperium-fourth-edition/) | Gradually revealed public objectives, private objectives, transactions, and promissory notes overlay military, political, and economic action. | Establishes the benchmark's mixed incentive architecture. It does not causally identify which layer creates hope, aggression, betrayal, or alliance change. |
| [“King Me”](https://www.gdcvault.com/play/1025683/board-game-design-day-king) and [*Oath* Designer Diary 8](https://ledergames.com/blogs/news/oath-designer-diary-8-destinations-and-paths-victory-part-3) | Kingmaking and interdependence are treated as design material; *Oath* gives current actions consequences for a continuing chronicle. | Establishes a coherent expert design philosophy and a concrete precedent. It remains designer testimony, not population evidence. |

The useful lesson is not to copy one mechanism. These games make different promises about betrayal, coalition duration, shared victory, and what losing means. Their rulebooks help create table norms, but the norms still interact with incentives and prior relationships.

## Provisional design implications and discriminating tests

| Provisional implication | Evidence basis | Cheapest credible discriminator |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Design for motive pluralism, not fixed player personas. | Board-gamer motivations, social-value orientation, conditional cooperation, and framing. | In representative sessions, ask players to rank motives before play and explain pivotal moves afterward; compare stated motives with behavior without treating either as ground truth alone. |
| Preserve honest uncertainty while keeping lagging players competent and consequential. | Suspense and competence studies; counterevidence from ego-involved persistence and adaptive goal disengagement. | Compare a control with a version adding consequential, self-owned post-victory goals. Measure enjoyment, perceived competence, decision quality, and spoiler behavior—not just continued participation. |
| Make alliance history and transition permission visible; do not assume automatic expiry is the answer. | Loyalty, conditional cooperation, group identity and its counterevidence; *Dune* and *Cosmic Encounter* precedents. | In a minimal multi-system sandbox, compare continuous renegotiation with scheduled public renewal. Track alliance churn, profitable-but-refused defections, perceived betrayal, and whether loyalty remains meaningful. |
| Treat objective cover as two independent questions: does it cause aggression, and does it change attribution? | Framing, blame, and moral-wiggle-room evidence. | Hold payoffs and strategic context constant while varying objective/role framing. Measure action frequency, perceived choice, attributed intent, blame, and post-action warmth separately for actors and targets. |
| Specify acceptable kingmaking before attempting to suppress or reward it. | No direct empirical verdict; multiple plausible behavioral mechanisms; opposing first-party philosophies. | Present target groups with concrete endgame cases, then test those cases in an integrated game. Distinguish own-position improvement, earned leverage, in-game retaliation, coalition obligation, and extra-game favoritism. |
| Sample tables, not only individuals. | Friendship increased coordinated punishment; identity effects depended on observable history. | Include intact friend groups and newly assembled groups, record prior relationships, and repeat sessions so conventions and reputations can develop. |
| Promote social claims only through integrated evidence. | Every focal outcome depends on incentives, attribution, history, and strategic response. | Use local probes to reject levers, then test promising ones in a minimal interaction sandbox and eventually repeated whole-game sessions, following the [evidence ladder](./emergence-aware-tabletop-prototyping.md). |

## Falsifying and counterevidence to keep visible

Any design thesis built from this research should be revised if later evidence contradicts it. These findings already block several overclaims:

- **Losing does not imply behavioral disengagement:** losers in one competition study showed more ego-involved persistence. ([Vansteenkiste and Deci, 2003](https://www.selfdeterminationtheory.org/SDT/documents/2003_MaartenDeci.pdf))
- **Disengagement is not always a design failure:** abandoning an unattainable goal and reengaging elsewhere can be adaptive. ([Wrosch et al., 2003](https://www.cmu.edu/dietrich/psychology/pdf/scales/GAS_article.pdf))
- **Frames are not reliable overrides:** label effects vanished with sequential play and in games whose material best responses opposed the prosocial frame. ([Ellingsen et al., 2012](https://scholar.harvard.edu/sites/scholar.harvard.edu/files/jmollers/files/1-s2.0-s0899825612000802-main.pdf); [Isaksen, Brekke, and Richter, 2019](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jeem.2018.11.005))
- **Identity does not automatically create loyalty:** an induced group identity had no effect when behavior and history were unobservable. ([Camera and Hohl, 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jebo.2021.06.017))
- **The standard betrayal-aversion mechanism is disputed:** aversion to delegating control and a sample-specific gender effect complicated the usual interpretation. ([Humphrey and Mondorf, 2021](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.econlet.2020.109663))
- **Punishment is not necessarily prosocial:** reanalysis and cross-cultural experiments found equality-seeking and punishment of high contributors, sometimes enough to erase punishment's cooperation benefit. ([Fowler, Johnson, and Smirnov, 2005](https://doi.org/10.1038/nature03256); [Herrmann, Thöni, and Gächter, 2008](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18323447/))
- **Hobbyist survey averages are not the target table:** they neither identify intact mixed-motivation groups nor show within-person motive switching. ([Cross et al., 2023](https://doi.org/10.1177/10468781231189493))
- **Designer testimony is not causal validation:** *Dune*, *Cosmic Encounter*, *Diplomacy*, *Twilight Imperium*, and *Oath* are precedents and philosophies, not controlled comparisons.

## Evidence limits and unresolved questions

No source located here directly studies repeated, multi-hour strategy games played by intact mixed-motivation friend groups while measuring alliance history, perceived defeat, betrayal attribution, kingmaking, and postgame relationship effects together. Consequently:

- laboratory games simplify incentives, duration, communication, and history;
- digital one-versus-one competition does not reproduce physical multiplayer negotiation;
- many samples are students or convenience samples, while the board-gamer survey is self-selected;
- the factory loyalty study and cross-societal punishment studies improve cultural breadth but do not establish tabletop transfer;
- continued action, self-reported enjoyment, perceived competence, blame, and relationship warmth are different outcomes and should not substitute for one another;
- no direct evidence located here establishes that objective requirements reduce interpersonal blame, that alliance expiry creates fluid politics, or that secondary goals reduce disengagement without increasing kingmaking;
- no direct empirical result resolves which kingmaking norms this project's target groups will accept;
- no isolated study can establish how these mechanisms behave when objectives, economy, geography, combat, negotiation, hidden information, and session length overlay one another.

The remaining thesis should therefore use bounded language: **research-supported mechanism**, **first-party precedent**, **target-table hypothesis**, or **integration-unproven**. The most important next evidence is not another universal claim; it is a set of discriminating interaction tests with the intended tables, followed by whole-game validation once a coherent architecture exists.
