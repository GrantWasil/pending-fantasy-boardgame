# Fantasy as structural design material

Research for **Investigate fantasy as structural design material**

Status: comparative design research, not a recommendation to adopt a setting or mechanism

## Executive answer

Fantasy becomes structural design material when a fictional fact is also a rule fact: it changes what a player is, what actions or information they possess, how geography behaves, what outcomes are valuable, or why players need and threaten one another. The strongest formulation found in these cases is:

> Because **this fantastic thing is true in the world**, a player can or must **do this mechanically**, which makes another player **respond differently**.

The clearest examples are not the games with the most lore.

- In *Spirit Island*, players are distributed spirits of places and natural processes. Presence, range, elements, tempo, and even which spaces count as usable land can follow from a spirit's identity. This creates cooperative division of labor across shared geography.[^spirit]
- In *War of the Ring*, the fantastic source material supplies two conflicts at incompatible scales: a public continental war and a hidden quest by a tiny Fellowship. Both players must allocate actions across those fronts, and each front changes the urgency of the other.[^wotr]
- In *Vast: The Crystal Caverns*, a player can be the Cave itself. The map is therefore not neutral terrain but a self-interested participant in a directed ecology of hunter, prey, parasite, monster, and environment.[^vast]
- In *Blood Rage*, glorious death, Valhalla, and Ragnarök invert ordinary wargame values: losing figures can score, dead figures return, and the announced destruction of provinces progressively compresses the battlefield.[^blood]
- In *Chaos in the Old World*, players embody gods whose different natures produce different victory incentives, target preferences, and foils, while the world can outlast and defeat everyone.[^chaos]

Other cases establish a boundary. *Terra Mystica* ties peoples to home terrain and makes proximity both obstructive and productive, but its economic-geographic architecture proved portable into *Gaia Project*.[^terra] *Small World* is explicitly a fantasy successor to the non-fantasy *Vinci*; fantasy gives its combinatorial identities a vivid vocabulary, but did not originate the core rise-and-decline system.[^smallworld] *Lords of Waterdeep* uses fantasy professions as colored resources consumed by contracts called Quests; the official rules do not give Clerics, Fighters, Rogues, or Wizards class-specific agency.[^waterdeep]

The conclusion is therefore bounded:

1. **Fantasy can be load-bearing, but “fantasy” alone predicts nothing.** The causal work comes from a particular ontology, value inversion, topology, capability grammar, or world process.
2. **Most strong examples are not uniquely fantasy-only.** Horror or science fiction could also support a living environment, nonhuman player, or impossible geography. The relevant affordance is often speculative ontology rather than medieval-fantasy imagery.
3. **A portable mechanism is not necessarily a failed theme.** Fantasy may still make radical asymmetry or exceptions learnable, emotionally coherent, and memorable. That is enabling or framing work, distinct from structural necessity.
4. **First-party rules establish procedures, not player psychology.** These sources do not prove that fantasy inherently softens aggression, improves diplomacy, creates immersion, or makes spectacle meaningful. Those remain target-table hypotheses.
5. **The useful prototype target is a coupled claim, not a skin test.** Test a small interaction ecology in which the fantastic premise changes several connected decisions. An isolated “magic power” cannot establish that fantasy is shaping the whole game.

## Method and terms

Cases were selected for structural variation rather than popularity: cooperative spatial defense, dual-scale war, total role asymmetry, economic terraforming, death-positive conflict, deity competition, identity-constrained deck construction, civilization rise-and-decline, and worker placement.

The factual descriptions come from official rulebooks, publisher pages, and first-party designer accounts. Rules are treated as evidence of procedure. Designer diaries are evidence of goals, development history, and reported problems—not causal proof of player experience. Interpretations below are marked as inferences where necessary.

“Reskin” is used narrowly. If the names and images can change while legal actions, payoffs, information, topology, timing, and player relationships remain intact, fantasy is not mechanically load-bearing in that implementation. No mechanism is literally impossible to abstract or retheme, so the practical question is **how much systemic rewriting a new fiction requires**.

This produces four useful levels. They are not a quality ranking.

| Level | Diagnostic | Typical contribution | Examples here |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Load-bearing in the implementation** | Removing the fantastic premise requires rewriting state transitions, roles, or relationships. | New kinds of agency, geography, lifecycle, or victory. | *Spirit Island*, *War of the Ring*, *Vast*, *Blood Rage* |
| **Enabling grammar** | The mechanism can travel, but fantasy makes extreme asymmetry or bounded exceptions coherent and inferable. | Capability limits, distinct imperatives, memorable counterplay. | *Chaos in the Old World*, *Terra Mystica*, *Magic: The Gathering—Commander* |
| **Framing or mnemonic support** | Procedures survive intact, but fantasy helps players parse, personify, or emotionally frame them. | Identity, tone, semantic compression, spectacle. | Much of *Small World*; parts of all cases |
| **Decorative or weakly coupled** | Fantastic nouns do not reliably predict permissions, payoffs, or relationships. | Setting flavor and product identity. | Much of *Lords of Waterdeep*'s adventurer-resource layer |

## Detailed findings

### 1. *Spirit Island*: a player is a relationship to place

The publisher describes players as different spirits of the land with distinct elemental powers. Eric Reuss's own overview makes the systemic coupling explicit: spirits spread Presence, use Presence for range, combine card elements to trigger innate effects, and differ in the speed and nature of their powers. Invaders advance through a semi-predictable Explore–Build–Ravage pipeline; Blight records damage to the island; generating Fear both produces effects and progressively relaxes the victory requirement.[^spirit]

This does two kinds of structural work. First, identity governs **how** a player can help: fast disruption, slow defense, movement, Fear, damage, restoration, or altered spatial access. Second, the shared island makes those specialties interdependent. A spirit is not merely an army with a special power; it is a distributed capability whose reach and growth are inscribed on common geography. Ocean's Hungry Grasp is the extreme example: while its Presence permits it, ocean spaces become usable lands for spirit rules, and moved Invaders can drown there.[^spirit-ocean]

The relationship produced is cooperative complementarity under forecast pressure. Players can see where the automated enemy will later Build or Ravage, but cannot solve every land independently; they exchange energy, range, movement, defense, and timing across boards. Fantasy is load-bearing because it supports an agent that can be geographically dispersed, locally strong, and categorically unlike the human societies on the board.

There are important limits. The same structure could be rethemed around planetary intelligences, weather systems, or network processes. More importantly, nonhuman player agency leaves the Dahan as nonplayer pieces. Reuss has publicly discussed the abstraction and the limited proactivity this creates, and has treated the game's relationship to Indigenous histories as an ongoing responsibility.[^spirit-dahan] Fantasy perspective can invert a colonial-game convention; it does not automatically resolve representational or agency problems.

### 2. *War of the Ring*: fantasy couples incompatible scales

The official rules and product description give the Shadow superior military force and two routes to victory: conquest or corruption of the Ring-bearers. The Free Peoples can win militarily, but their defining route is moving the hidden Fellowship to Mount Doom while delaying conquest. Nations do not begin equally ready for war, named characters alter political and action possibilities, and the Hunt makes repeated Fellowship movement increasingly dangerous.[^wotr]

The structural material is not “there are Orc miniatures” or even “there is magic.” It is the source story's **dual scale**. One board state supports an open territorial war and a partly hidden journey whose location, exposure, companions, and Corruption obey different rules. Action dice force both players to choose which scale receives attention. Shadow armies can win before the quest finishes; spending too much effort on conquest can leave the Fellowship unchecked; Free Peoples military resistance matters even when it is strategically doomed because time is a resource for the quest.

This produces pursuit, diversion, and cross-front opportunity costs rather than fluid alliances. In team play, players divide responsibility within fixed blocs, but the political structure remains permanent antagonism. The game therefore offers strong evidence for fantasy-shaped conflict and uncertainty, and weak evidence for negotiation or shifting coalitions.

The engine is not setting-locked. The designers have reused action-dice ideas elsewhere, and a hidden courier, weapon, or insurgent network could reproduce parts of the quest.[^wotr-dice] A successful reskin would nevertheless need to replace the whole coupled architecture—massive force disparity, reluctant mobilization, uncertain pursuit, character intervention, moral attrition, and two differently scaled clocks—not merely rename the Ring.

### 3. *Vast*: the environment can be a political actor

*Vast* assigns players categorically different roles. The Knight develops movement, perception, and strength; Goblins manage tribes, Rage, monsters, and secrets; the Dragon converts Sloth into Wakefulness; the Thief steals and upgrades; the Cave places, rotates, reconnects, and eventually removes tiles as it collapses. Each role has a different objective and action system.[^vast]

The most consequential fantasy move is making the Cave a player. Geography now has preferences, tempo, hidden information, and a win condition. Other players cannot treat the map as a neutral container: its expansion creates access and danger, and its collapse changes what survival and pursuit mean. Combined with directed objectives—one role hunts another while a third races to escape—the result is an ecology of non-equivalent relationships rather than an all-against-all contest.

This is load-bearing speculative ontology. A mundane army-faction skin would not explain why one participant controls topology while another manages a single hero and another a dispersed horde. A haunted house, station AI, or living planet could, so the evidence supports fantasy broadly as permission for ontological asymmetry rather than this genre uniquely.

That permission is expensive. Leder's later design writing describes radical asymmetry as both a source of interesting player relationships and a cost paid in rules and headaches; development retrospectives also note that a role can become mechanically elaborate yet feel like a separate game.[^vast-cost] Fantasy can justify different rulebooks, but justification does not guarantee interaction. Every role still needs consequential interfaces with the others.

### 4. *Terra Mystica*: habitat creates productive rivalry

The official rulebook begins with the structural claim: every faction is bound to a specific home terrain and can build only there, so expansion requires terraforming. It then directly identifies the central relationship: building near opponents limits free expansion but causes neighboring players to gain Power; proximity also makes Trading Houses cheaper.[^terra]

Fantasy therefore connects identity, capability, and geography. A faction's habitat affects the cost and direction of expansion, while faction abilities—flying, tunneling, river access, or altered conversion—change how it navigates that landscape. Because terrain conversion is persistent, one player's growth transforms another's opportunity space. Neighbors are simultaneously competitors, infrastructure discounts, and sources or recipients of Power.

This is meaningful structural loading, but not fantasy necessity. The same production tracks, network building, terrain conversion, and adjacency exchange can be expressed as ecology, industry, or planetary settlement; *Gaia Project* is first-party evidence of that portability.[^terra-gaia] Fantasy's strongest contribution is semantic compression: “Witches fly” or “Dwarves tunnel” makes exceptions easier to learn and infer. It contributes little to hidden information, negotiation, or world agency.

### 5. *Blood Rage*: mythology changes the sign of loss

The official rules award Glory for battles, pillage, quests, and valiant death. They announce which province Ragnarök will destroy, award escalating Glory for each figure destroyed there, place those figures in Valhalla, then return all figures from Valhalla to their owners after the Age. Later Ages therefore occur on less territory. The rules' own strategy guidance says a suicidal battle can be profitable and can also deplete the winner's hand.[^blood]

This is a strong fantasy-system coupling because three mythic propositions operate together:

1. honorable death has value;
2. the dead can return from Valhalla; and
3. the world is visibly ending.

Together they change combat interpretation. Committing to a doomed province may be scoring rather than a mistake. Losing a battle may advance a drafted Loki strategy while forcing the winner to spend a valuable card. The shrinking map also turns apocalypse into pacing and increasing spatial pressure. Opponents must infer not only whether a player can win a battle, but whether they want to.

The reskin test is demanding but not impossible: evacuation, martyrdom, cloning, or cyclical rebirth could produce similar incentives. A replacement must preserve the connection among sacrifice, return, and map destruction. Renaming only the units would lose the causal coherence.

The miniatures and monsters add spectacle, and CMON describes art and components as sources of drama and narrative information.[^blood-spectacle] But the sources do not show that component size changes strategy. Ragnarök is structurally spectacular because the board state disappears and incentives change; a large Frost Giant is mechanically structural only to the extent that its rules, cost, and threat matter.

### 6. *Chaos in the Old World*: personality becomes an imperative

Each player is a Ruinous Power with a unique deck, upgrades, followers, and Threat-dial condition, alongside a shared route through corruption and domination. The Old World produces events and resistance, and if its deck expires before a Chaos player wins, all players lose.[^chaos]

Eric Lang's designer notes are unusually direct. He wanted players connected to their characters, the world to feel alive, and each god's alternate victory path to reflect its nature while intersecting with the others rather than creating four solitaire games. He reports that removing Khorne reduces combat and removing Tzeentch reduces dynamic movement and predictability. He also encoded opposing divine relationships through card interactions rather than hard matchup bonuses.[^chaos-notes]

Fantasy here creates **imperative asymmetry**. Khorne seeks kills in multiple regions; Nurgle values populous regions; Tzeentch tracks magical concentrations and manipulation; Slaanesh values noble and heroic targets. Consequently, another player's pieces can be prey for one god, competition for another, and an inconvenient distraction for a third. The world clock supplies common pressure without making the players allies.

The boundary is equally instructive. Lang's public diary says the crucial hook was giving each player a unique personality; the fact that these were famous fantasy icons was “icing.”[^chaos-diary] The implementation is structurally fantastical because players are gods with world-scale imperatives, but the specific IP is replaceable. The larger warning is ecological fragility: if removing one role changes combat and movement, balance and experience depend on the whole role set.

### 7. *Commander*: a hero bounds hidden possibility

In *Magic: The Gathering*'s Commander format, each player publicly chooses a legendary commander, builds a singleton deck around its color identity, and can repeatedly cast it from the command zone at an increasing cost. The official format is normally multiplayer free-for-all.[^commander]

Mark Rosewater describes the commander as the face and personality of a deck, grounding the theme despite singleton variance. He separately describes the color pie as a system in which flavor dictates what mechanics a color can and cannot use, creating both personality and built-in weaknesses.[^commander-design][^color-pie]

That produces a useful interaction between public and hidden identity. The commander and colors disclose a capability envelope while most specific cards remain uncertain. **Inference:** opponents can negotiate and assess threats using an identity that predicts some strengths and absences without revealing the player's exact hand. The persistent hero also ensures that a variable deck can repeatedly express its declared plan.

This is enabling grammar, not evidence that fantasy is indispensable. The same color structure now supports many genres and licensed settings. What is load-bearing is a public, personified identity that restricts private capability and remains mechanically accessible. Fantasy supplies an enormous vocabulary of heroes, creatures, magic, and exceptions, but the causal architecture is identity plus constraint.

### 8. *Small World* and *Lords of Waterdeep*: two boundaries

*Small World* publicly pairs one fantasy Race with one Special Power, creating a temporary capability package. Players conquer with it until overextension makes Decline attractive, then select a new combination while remnants of the old civilization remain. Fantasy helps bizarre combinations read as compact identities and permits powers such as a Dragon or Sorcerers without a naturalistic simulation.[^smallworld]

Yet the publisher explicitly presents *Small World* as a fantasy follow-up to Philippe Keyaerts's *Vinci*. That genealogy is unusually strong counterevidence to a claim of structural necessity. The rise, conquest, overextension, decline, and replacement cycle predates the fantasy presentation. Fantasy is doing mnemonic, tonal, and combinatorial work; the engine does not depend on it.

*Lords of Waterdeep* is an even lower-loading case. Agents occupy action spaces; four kinds of adventurer are wooden cubes; Quests specify a recipe of adventurers and Gold, return those resources to supply, and grant rewards; hidden Lords award bonus points for Quest categories.[^waterdeep] Some cards create thematic exceptions, but the core relationship remains competition over spaces, resources, buildings, and secret scoring.

**Inference:** replacing Clerics, Fighters, Rogues, and Wizards with four goods, and Quests with contracts, leaves most state transitions intact. This does not mean the D&D setting has no experiential value. It means the rulebook offers little evidence that the adventurers' fantasy identities determine what those resources can do.

## Structural patterns and the relationships they produce

### Fantasy is strongest when it changes the unit of agency

A conventional strategy-game player is often a state, army, or firm. Fantasy permits a player to be a distributed spirit, immortal god, living Cave, singular hero, horde, or corrupting force. That can create genuinely non-isomorphic relationships:

| Structural move | Relationship produced | Cases | Characteristic risk |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Distributed natural identity | Cooperative specialization across shared space | *Spirit Island* | Coordination burden; human groups may lose agency in the representation |
| Environment as player | Host–intruder and topology-control relationships | *Vast* | A role can become a separate subsystem with weak interfaces |
| Divine imperative | Predator, foil, and opportunist relations that vary by opponent | *Chaos in the Old World* | Player-count fragility and high learning cost |
| Public hero plus bounded capabilities | Identity-conditioned threat assessment and bargaining | *Commander* | Stereotyped evaluation; card-pool and knowledge asymmetry |

The lesson is not “make every faction stranger.” It is that a different ontology matters only when other players must change their behavior in response.

### Fantasy can couple systems at different scales

*War of the Ring* connects armies, nations, characters, hidden movement, and individual Corruption. *Spirit Island* connects local Presence and powers to island-wide Fear and Blight. *Blood Rage* connects a single battle's losses to Valhalla, Age-level scoring, and progressive map destruction. These systems produce stories because one event can be read simultaneously at several scales.

This supports the earlier finding that emergence comes from consequential coupling, not mechanic count. Fantasy is valuable when it supplies a coherent reason for those scales to coexist. A deck of unrelated magical exceptions can increase content without producing the same causal legibility.

### Geography can be actor, medium, or value—not backdrop

The cases support three stronger uses of fantastic geography:

- **Actor:** the Cave chooses how the map grows and collapses.
- **Medium of identity:** spirit Presence and terrain affinity determine where powers or construction are possible.
- **Changing value field:** Ragnarök removes provinces, while terraforming permanently changes who can build efficiently.

Each use changes player relationships. Players negotiate or coordinate over spatial access, feed Power to neighbors, plan around environmental intent, or contest land that is doomed precisely because destruction has value.

### Myth can change the value function

Fantasy is unusually good at making ordinary losses ambiguous. Death can yield Glory and return from Valhalla; fear can matter more than physical destruction; corruption can defeat a militarily successful side; a god may prefer widespread small killings to conventional territorial control.

Value inversion creates uncertainty about intent, not only outcome. It can enrich conflict, but it also raises policing and onboarding costs. Players must understand why an apparently losing action could be rational. If only the acting player knows the fantasy exception, the system may feel arbitrary rather than emergent.

### A capability grammar is more useful than unrestricted magic

The color pie, a spirit's elemental affinities, a god's imperative, or a faction's terrain relationship all answer both “what can this identity do?” and “what can it not do?” Constraints create counterplay. By contrast, magic treated as permission for any card to break any rule erodes inference.

A structurally useful fantasy grammar therefore needs:

- public or learnable categories;
- characteristic strengths and absences;
- repeated interaction with shared systems; and
- enough consistency that opponents can plan before seeing a specific exception.

### Spectacle is structural only when it carries state

Spectacle can still be worthwhile as art direction, but it should be classified accurately.

- Ragnarök removing a province is state-bearing spectacle: topology, capacity, scoring, and future conflict all change.
- A Cave physically collapsing or a board accumulating Blight and Presence communicates the world's changing agency.
- A large monster miniature is primarily presentation unless its size reliably communicates a distinct rule, threat, or constraint.

Publisher claims about drama and immersion establish intent. They do not establish that spectacle changes decisions or social attribution.

## Cosmetic-failure tests

Use these tests on any proposed fantasy element before calling it structural.

1. **Complete the causal sentence.** “Because ___ is true in the fiction, the player can or must ___, causing another player or system to ___.” If the last clause is missing, the element is probably isolated chrome.
2. **Perform the noun-swap.** Replace every fantastic noun with neutral labels. If permissions, payoffs, information, and strategy remain obvious and intact, the fantasy layer is framing or decoration.
3. **Trace a capability and a weakness.** A fantasy identity that only grants bonuses is content. A structural identity changes what is possible and impossible, giving opponents something to infer and exploit.
4. **Check relationship topology.** Does the premise change who needs, fears, feeds, pursues, protects, or polices whom? If every opponent remains generically interchangeable, it has not materially changed politics.
5. **Check geography.** Does the premise change adjacency, access, range, terrain value, map growth, or map destruction? A fantasy map with ordinary spaces is still an ordinary topology.
6. **Check persistence and coupling.** Does the element touch at least two core systems across multiple turns, or is it a one-use card effect? Repeated coupling is stronger evidence of structural material.
7. **Audit the exception grammar.** Can players predict classes of magical effects from public identity, or must they memorize every card? Surprise alone is not a coherent uncertainty system.
8. **Remove the spectacle.** Replace miniatures and art with labeled tokens. If decisions and inference are unchanged, spectacle is experiential support, not mechanical structure. Test the presentation later on its own terms.
9. **Inspect the design's ancestry.** *Small World* following *Vinci* and *Gaia Project* following *Terra Mystica* show when an engine survives a setting change. That does not erase theme, but it narrows the structural claim.
10. **Separate social claims from rules claims.** If the hypothesis is that monsters make aggression feel less personal or magic increases wonder, measure attribution, blame, engagement, and recall with the intended tables. A rulebook cannot prove those effects.

## Implications for this design exploration

### Treat fantasy as a hypothesis generator, not a settled genre package

The promising questions are specific:

- What if players are not all the same kind of political entity?
- What if territory is alive, partisan, transformable, or partly controlled by a player?
- What if defeat, death, oath, memory, or transformation changes the value of conflict?
- What if a public magical identity constrains private capabilities enough to support inference and negotiation?
- What if visible geopolitical conflict overlays a smaller hidden or spiritual conflict with a different clock?

“Add spells,” “use fantasy factions,” or “include monsters” are not yet structural hypotheses.

### Prototype a coupled fantasy statement

The user's concern about isolated prototypes is supported by these cases. The Cave matters because every other role traverses it. Ragnarök matters because it changes scoring, lifecycle, space, and combat motives. The Fellowship matters because it competes with the whole war for actions and time. Testing any of those as a detached rule would miss its causal work.

The appropriate next unit is therefore a **small interaction ecology**, consistent with the project's [emergence-aware evidence ladder](./emergence-aware-tabletop-prototyping.md): one fantastic ontology or world process, two or three connected systems, and enough shared state for players to adapt to one another. Local probes can reject unusable interfaces, but promotion requires integrated play.

### Keep the structural and experiential evaluations separate

Run a gray-box version to determine whether the fantasy premise actually changes decisions and relationships. Then test whether names, art, scale, and material presentation improve comprehension, emotional tone, recall, and spectacle. A result can be mechanically portable yet experientially transformed by fantasy; those are two different successes.

### Prefer bounded impossibility over arbitrary exception

The strongest cases make one impossible proposition generative:

- the land has minds;
- a place can be a player;
- death is temporary and glorious;
- one artifact creates a second scale of war;
- gods value different forms of ruin.

Each proposition yields several predictable consequences. This is a stronger foundation than a broad magic system whose primary function is to authorize exceptions.

### Do not assume fantasy solves politics

*War of the Ring* fixes moral blocs. *Spirit Island* is cooperative. *Terra Mystica* has almost no direct negotiation. *Vast* can produce temporary alignments but also role isolation. *Chaos in the Old World* creates asymmetric foils, not diplomatic institutions. These cases show that fantasy can reshape incentives and relationships; they do not show that it automatically produces shifting coalitions, meaningful play from behind, or socially safe aggression.

Those political outcomes still need the interdependencies, leverage, commitment rules, and recovery roles identified in [Political-system families beyond *Twilight Imperium*](./political-system-families-beyond-ti.md).

## Evidence limits

- This is a structural precedent review, not a controlled comparison of themed and unthemed versions.
- Rulebooks establish what players may do, not which emotions or stories they actually experience.
- Designer diaries can accurately report intent and development observations while still reflecting selective memory, prototypes, or one studio's playtest culture.
- Several “fantasy” affordances are more accurately speculative-fiction affordances. A living Cave, distributed spirit, or divine imperative can be rewritten as horror or science fiction while preserving the ontology.
- Licensed worlds such as Middle-earth provide unusually dense prior knowledge. Their results may depend on audience familiarity and cannot be generalized to an original setting without testing.
- High asymmetry makes causal attribution difficult: theme, role rules, balance, player order, knowledge, and group policing change together.
- No source here establishes that fantasy framing reduces interpersonal blame, makes aggression feel game-legitimized, or improves engagement for a mixed-motivation friend group.
- No isolated case resolves how these fantasy structures behave when negotiation, economy, combat, hidden information, geography, and long-session arcs are integrated in this project.

The safe conclusion is therefore: **fantasy is promising structural material when one impossible premise repeatedly constrains agency and changes shared relationships. Whether that premise belongs in this game remains an integration question, not a solved design decision.**
