# Epic scale across different runtimes

Research for [Investigate epic scale across different runtimes](https://github.com/GrantWasil/pending-fantasy-boardgame/issues/7), conducted 2026-07-20.  
Status: comparative design research, not a runtime or mechanics recommendation

## Executive answer

The cases do not support a single recipe for making a shorter *Twilight Imperium*. They support three different architectures for scale:

1. **Accumulated epic** keeps many consequential systems in one long session. *Twilight Imperium: Fourth Edition* lets territory, fleets, technology, trade, objectives, faction powers, and laws develop together over a publisher-estimated four to eight hours.[^ti-product] Its scale comes partly from the amount of history the table has time to accumulate.
2. **Consequence-dense epic** narrows the game to a few highly leveraged decisions. *Dune* and *Pax Pamir: Second Edition* load politics, identity, and sudden reversals into faction powers, hidden information, allegiance, and punctuated victory checks rather than simulating every dimension of an empire. Their intensity is not evidence that all of *Twilight Imperium*'s experiential breadth can be preserved in less time.
3. **Distributed epic** moves historical scale outside one sitting. *Arcs* divides a bounded campaign into episodes and uses intermissions for transformations that would interrupt ordinary play; *Oath* lets the map, deck, and victory system remember consequences across an open-ended chronicle.[^arcs-time][^oath-origins] These games transform the commitment instead of simply reducing it.

Across all three, shorter runtime is purchased through specific exchanges:

- **Simultaneity buys throughput but reduces shared attention.** *7 Wonders* represents three ages of civilizational development in thirty minutes by having players choose and resolve cards simultaneously. Conflict is then an automatic comparison with adjacent players.[^seven-product][^seven-rules] This preserves breadth of subject and visible growth while providing little support for table-wide politics or a jointly witnessed dramatic sequence.
- **Punctuated scoring makes a short history legible.** Stronghold checks, dominance checks, declared ambitions, and age boundaries convert a continuous simulation into a few consequential public moments.
- **Role-loaded asymmetry compresses exposition and motive.** A faction permission can simultaneously communicate identity, strategic incentive, and a reason for another player to bargain or attack. It is efficient, but increases teaching, matchup, and evaluation costs.
- **Political richness depends on protected bargaining structure, not merely permission to talk.** Classic *Dune* spends rules and time on auctions, binding bribes, hidden traitors, and sandworm-gated alliances. Its sixty-minute sibling keeps storm, spice, strongholds, traitors, battle wheels, and faction differences, but drops the classic alliance and auction phases and halves the maximum turn count.[^dune-rules][^dune-short-rules][^dune-short-design] It is direct evidence that a design can preserve tactical tension and setting while sacrificing much of its formal political ecology.
- **Material and spatial spectacle are not interchangeable with narrative scope.** A game can cover centuries quickly without producing a long buildup, large shared map transformation, or remembered diplomatic history.

The practical conclusion for this project is **not** that an epic target requires a four-to-eight-hour game. It is that “epic” must first be separated into emotional, political, narrative, and spectacular aims. A runtime choice determines which of those can accumulate during play, which must be concentrated into high-leverage events, which can be displaced between sessions, and which will be lost. That is a design-philosophy question still to explore, not a solved specification.

## What this comparison can and cannot show

Here, four channels are kept distinct:

- **Emotional scale:** commitment, vulnerability, reversals, and outcomes that feel consequential.
- **Political scale:** interdependence, bargaining, coalition change, legitimacy, and contested shared institutions.
- **Narrative scale:** a causally intelligible before-and-after that players can reconstruct as a story.
- **Spectacular scale:** visible accumulation and transformation across a shared material space.

The matrix reports **rule support** for those channels, not proof that a game reliably achieves an experience. Sources are publisher rules, product specifications, and first-party designer/developer accounts. A publisher calling a game “epic” establishes positioning, not player experience. A designer diary establishes intent, development observations, or a documented trade-off, not a controlled causal result. Interpretive claims below are therefore marked as inferences.

Published times are planning estimates rather than observed distributions. No first-party minute estimate was located for classic *Dune*, so its row uses the rules' ten-turn cap and does not invent one. Diaries about *Arcs* sometimes describe in-development structures; current product rules and specifications take precedence for final procedure.

## What each architecture is actually doing

### 1. *Twilight Imperium*: scale through co-accumulation

*Twilight Imperium* does not merely contain many subsystems. Its rules repeatedly make them refer to the same developing state. Planets supply resources and influence; command tokens constrain fleets and strategic participation; technology changes capabilities; objectives redirect expansion; commodities become useful through exchange; controlling Mecatol Rex activates a lawmaking phase; laws can then alter future action.[^ti-announcement] Strategy cards also give their owner a primary action and other players a weaker secondary action, so an individual choice can create a table-wide timing question.[^ti-strategy]

**Inference:** the long runtime buys time for identities and dependencies to acquire history. A trade partner can later become a military threat; a fleet can be both deterrent and objective instrument; a law can reinterpret an already developed position. The resulting “epic” quality is therefore partly a property of repeated cross-system reuse. Cutting minutes while keeping a checklist of fleets, trade, politics, research, and objectives would not necessarily preserve that quality if those systems no longer have time to change one another's meaning.

The design nevertheless uses compression. Public objectives produce a ten-point race instead of requiring literal conquest. Strategy-card secondaries distribute activity across another player's turn. A central political threshold—occupation of Mecatol Rex—delays agendas until the map has developed. These are clocks and phase gates inside a long game, not evidence that duration alone creates scale.

### 2. The two *Dune* games: the clearest preservation/sacrifice pair

Classic *Dune* puts a remarkable amount of leverage on a small number of objects. Victory depends on a few strongholds. Battles use simultaneous dials that require players to commit how many forces they will lose even if they win. Traitors can reverse an apparently secure battle. Faction advantages place information, transport, money, prediction, and alternate victory in different hands.[^dune-rules][^dune-history]

Its politics are also procedural. A sandworm creates a Nexus, and only there may alliances form, change, or break. Allies share a harder territorial victory, can support bidding and shipment, and may privately exchange information. Outside alliances, public deals and bribes are binding within explicit asset limits.[^dune-rules] The system therefore spends both rules and session time making promises, timing, and partner choice materially consequential.

The designers describe *A Game of Conquest and Diplomacy* as a separate, more accessible sibling rather than a replacement. It keeps four factions, spice, storm, strongholds, traitors, battle wheels, and area control, adds a market deck, caps play at five turns, and supplies a points result if no one captures three strongholds by the end.[^dune-short-design][^dune-short-rules] Its seven phases contain neither the classic auction nor Nexus/alliance phase.

**Inference:** this is not “the same epic in half the time.” It is a different profile. The short game preserves setting, asymmetry, commitment, danger, and a visible fight for Arrakis. It compresses the arc with an earlier victory window and a hard fallback. It gives up a large portion of the formal bargaining economy and reduces the time over which alliances, grudges, resource circulation, and recovery can evolve. That may be an excellent exchange for a particular target, but it is an exchange.

### 3. *Pax Pamir*: generational scope through punctuated resets

*Pax Pamir* shows how to produce political scale without modeling a comprehensive state. Players are always loyal to one of three coalitions but compete personally over their influence inside it. Spies can compromise cards and enable betrayal; regional control can extract bribes; a loyalty change can discard investments tied to the former coalition.[^pamir-rules][^pamir-intelligence]

Up to four dominance checks create the arc. If one coalition has a commanding block lead, its loyal players compare influence. Otherwise players compare personal pieces in play. A successful check clears coalition blocks, allowing a new political configuration to emerge; an early score lead can also end the game.[^pamir-rules]

Designer Cole Wehrle describes this structure as giving the conflict a generational scope while enabling reversals and making players identify with recurring court cards. He also reports that the first edition's density of historical “chrome” overwhelmed attention; the second edition deliberately contained roughly half the rules while aiming to remain sharper rather than merely friendlier.[^pamir-theme][^pamir-second]

**Inference:** the compression method is selective abstraction plus repeated constitutional moments. The game keeps allegiance, leverage, and historical persons but gives up detailed technology, production, and force development. Its drama depends on players reading a shifting threshold and on a scoring event being important enough to reorganize everyone. This is a candidate architecture for political epic, not evidence that it supplies *Twilight Imperium*'s spectacular or developmental scale.

### 4. *Arcs*: one beat per episode, with change at the seams

The base game's multi-use action cards couple what a player may do with who controls tempo. A player can follow, pivot, copy, or spend an extra card to seize initiative; high cards tend to protect tempo while low cards yield more actions.[^arcs-action] Declared ambitions make players choose which public scoring contests matter during this game, and battles resolve with one roll rather than a separate lengthy minigame.[^arcs-product][^arcs-combat]

The campaign's more important compression is temporal. Wehrle's design account distinguishes ordinary “adventure time” from “world time” at an intermission. Instead of asking the core action system to depict every political transformation, an episode resolves roughly one story beat per player; the intermission can then branch fates, introduce card packets, alter rules, and maintain or transform the position for the next episode.[^arcs-time]

**Inference:** the break is doing mechanical work. It permits changes large enough to create an era boundary without stopping a live turn for new rules and exposition. It also lets a siege, resource cache, or hard-won ability matter later. The trade is continuity and total commitment: a two-hour episode is approachable, but the larger arc still requires a group to return, and each isolated session is deliberately narrower than a complete space opera.

### 5. *Oath*: a chronicle instead of a complete arc

*Oath* takes the cross-session strategy further. Its publisher describes each 45–120 minute game as changing resources, actions, and even a future victory condition.[^oath-product] The designer account is more specific: the game remembers part of its map, part of its deck of people and institutions, and how the prior game ended. A winner's territory forms the next map, while the map can re-center around a new capital.[^oath-map]

Within a game, political perspective also compresses other domains. Wehrle reports abandoning a more elaborate production system and using a limited “complexity budget.” Favor, warbands, and magic make economy legible as political capital, force, and secrets; card powers connect those resources to other systems.[^oath-economy] Combat was redesigned to resolve anything from a raid to a world campaign quickly while aligning its result with the game's emotional climax.[^oath-combat]

**Inference:** *Oath* does not make one short session contain generations. It makes each session an episode whose selected outcome changes what later episodes begin with. It preserves consequence and political memory while sacrificing comprehensive continuity: many personal resources reset, players can change, and a single play may feel like one unstable reign rather than an entire history. It is an answer to a different question from “how do we finish a *Twilight Imperium*-shaped arc by midnight?”

### 6. *7 Wonders*: a necessary counterexample

The publisher's rules and product page are unusually explicit about throughput. Players choose and apply cards simultaneously; play is divided into three Ages with stronger cards; military conflict is resolved against immediate neighbors at each Age's end; total score decides the winner after the third.[^seven-product][^seven-rules]

This structure can represent a city's growth across military, science, commerce, resources, civic construction, and a monumental Wonder in thirty minutes. Yet there is no shared territorial map, no table-wide diplomatic institution, and no negotiated war sequence in the base rules.

**Inference:** broad subject matter, an illustrated production, and escalating numbers can communicate historical scope, but they do not automatically create political history, mutual vulnerability, or a shared dramatic arc. Simultaneous action solves downtime partly by privatizing attention. This is the clearest warning against using “covers centuries” or “has many strategic domains” as a proxy for the target experience.

## Cross-case mechanisms and their costs

### Public punctuation

Every case makes time legible with shared thresholds: ten victory points, stronghold control, dominance checks, ambitions and chapters, regime succession, or three Ages. These moments do more than end play. They tell players when to reevaluate relationships and make the preceding activity narratively compressible.

**Design implication:** a shorter game needs fewer but more causally legible turning points. Adding more scoring intervals can increase bookkeeping without creating an arc. The useful question is whether each punctuation can change priorities, alliances, or the interpretation of the board.

### Consequence density

A short game cannot afford many actions that change only one private number. The strongest compact cases make a decision touch several structures: a *Pax Pamir* card affects court, map, actions, loyalty, and a coming check; a *Dune* battle risks forces, leaders, information, stronghold position, and alliance prospects; an *Arcs* card determines both action and tempo.

**Design implication:** consequence density is a plausible substitute for some duration, but it increases cognitive load and makes local changes propagate. It must be evaluated in an interaction sandbox and eventually a whole-game skeleton; an isolated mechanism can show local interest but cannot establish the resulting political ecology.

### Spatial concentration

Mecatol Rex, Dune's strongholds, and *Pax Pamir*'s six regions turn large political claims into contention over a small number of legible places. Concentration increases encounter rate and gives diplomacy concrete stakes without requiring a vast map.

**Trade-off:** too much concentration makes every game converge on the same scrum or lets a single threshold dominate all other stories. Spectacular geographic discovery and long-front strategy are reduced.

### Asymmetry as narrative compression

Strong faction powers pre-load motive and voice. The rules can tell a player “you profit from shipment,” “you know part of the battle,” or “your spies live in other courts” instead of waiting for an emergent economic identity to develop.

**Trade-off:** this borrows time from teaching and balance evaluation. It can also make the story feel assigned rather than earned. Emergent asymmetry, as in an *Arcs* campaign or changing *Oath* world, requires more play time but lets identity be historical rather than selected at setup.

### Temporal displacement

Intermissions and chronicles move disruptive or generational changes between ordinary turns or games. This protects session length and makes larger transformations possible.

**Trade-off:** elapsed campaign time is not reduced, only packaged differently. Group continuity, memory, storage, and the possibility of abandonment become design costs. A cross-session history should not be presented as a solution for players who specifically want a complete one-night arc.

### Simultaneity

Simultaneous action can make runtime relatively insensitive to player count. It is valuable for private development and routine choice.

**Trade-off:** politics and spectacle often require shared attention—seeing a threat form, bargaining about it, and witnessing commitment and consequence. Simultaneity should therefore be used selectively if the target includes table-wide political theater.

## Implications for this project

1. **Do not choose mechanics or runtime from the word “epic.”** First determine which channels the intended tables must experience and which can be weaker: emotional commitment, political realignment, remembered narrative, visible spectacle, or some explicit combination.
2. **Treat the temporal container as a thesis choice.** A complete long session, a consequence-dense shorter session, a bounded multi-session arc, and an open chronicle produce different kinds of scale. None is a neutral delivery format for the same design.
3. **Prototype with a thin integrated spine early.** To investigate an epic arc, even a crude prototype needs minimal forms of the systems that create its candidate causal chain—position, scarce action, conflict, interdependence, public stakes, and ending. Zoomed-in probes can then reject or tune levers, but the integrated spine must be replayed after each consequential change.
4. **Measure traces, not adjectives.** Candidate observations include: what public state changed; which decision altered another player's plan; whether a relationship reversed and why; whether stakes visibly escalated; which events players reconstruct afterward; and which promised channel never became relevant. “Felt epic” can remain a closing question, but it should not be the only evidence.
5. **Run temporal alternatives as competing hypotheses.** A compact one-session skeleton and a bounded episodic skeleton may share some materials, but they should be treated as different architectures with explicit predicted gains and losses—not as easy and advanced modes assumed to preserve one experience.
6. **Expect the key finding to be a sacrifice boundary.** The useful outcome may be “political history does not survive below this amount of bargaining time in this architecture,” or “spectacle survives, but only by reducing developmental breadth.” Discovering that boundary is progress, not failure.

## Evidence limits and unresolved questions

- The sources establish procedures, stated time ranges, design intent, and some first-party development observations. They do not provide controlled, comparable measures of emotional, political, narrative, or spectacular experience.
- Publisher time ranges vary with player count, familiarity, teach time, analysis, and table culture. They should not be treated as precise performance data.
- The games differ in intellectual property, art budget, component volume, audience, player count, and age. A licensed setting may supply narrative meaning before play that an original setting must earn at the table.
- Classic *Dune*'s rules provide a ten-turn cap but the first-party sources reviewed here do not provide a minute estimate. No secondary estimate has been substituted.
- The *Arcs* diaries are valuable records of design reasoning, but some describe an evolving prototype. Claims about current base-game procedure rely on current product specifications and rules.
- The apparent success of a market title does not identify which mechanism caused an experience, which players received it, or which players rejected it.
- This is not an exhaustive survey. It deliberately compares sharply different temporal architectures and includes *7 Wonders* as a scope-without-politics counterexample.
- The central unresolved questions belong to this project's intended players: Which epic channels matter to them? Must the arc complete in one sitting? How much political negotiation is welcome? How much personal history must accumulate before a reversal matters? What total and per-session commitment is acceptable?

The defensible frontier is therefore: **multiple architectures plausibly support different epic channels, but the target profile, temporal container, and acceptable sacrifices remain open.** Those choices should be investigated through integrated comparative prototypes rather than inferred from any one precedent.
